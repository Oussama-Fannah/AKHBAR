Morocco records 1,960 Covid-19 cases, 2 deaths

Morocco recorded 1,960 new Covid-19 cases on Thursday, raising the total of cases since the beginning of the outbreak to 961,058. The kingdom has also reported 2 Covid related deaths in the past 24 hours. As of Thursday, the ministry of health reported a total of 14,844 people who died from Covid-19. Morocco continues to bid on its national vaccination campaign, in an effort to contain the spread of the coronavirus. According to the ministry’s latest tally, 24,552,646 people received their first Covid-19 vaccine shot, while 22,924,662 were fully inoculated. Morocco has also administered a total of 2,932,157 booster shots, as of Thursday.

Morocco's government decides against extending school winter vacations

Government spokesperson Mustapha Baitas announced that school winter vacations will not be extended, despite rising COVID-19 infection rates. Speaking at the weekly press briefing following the government’s weekly government council this Thursday, December 30, the official stated that “there is no extension of the school vacation,” which is due to end this Sunday, January 2. On whether the Scientific Committee will present a recommendation to the government in order to extend the inter-school vacation, previous sources confirmed to Hespress AR that this is “currently excluded and there is no direction in this direction.”

Two members of the scientific committee had told Hespress AR that “this point is currently not being discussed, and no decision has been taken in order to extend the holiday.” They stressed that “students cannot be held responsible for the negligence and laxity of parents, youth and the elderly in adhering to the necessary precautions and in completing the stages of the vaccination process.” The members of the scientific committee added that “the students were vaccinated in a large proportion, as the number of vaccinated people reached about two million and six hundred thousand out of two million and eight hundred thousand, which means that they contributed to collective immunity, unlike adults.”

Morocco's education system remains undecided on conducting exams virtually or in-person

Morocco’s education system has yet to decide on whether exams will take place in-person or remotely, with the exam season fast approaching in mid-January. Morocco’s Minister of Higher Education, Abdelatif Miraoui, has urged all education establishments to conduct exams remotely until further notice. The official made the request to the heads of universities saying that “the country has not yet overcome the risk of a new COVID-19 wave.” However, a Hespress source from within the scientific committee confirmed that it is likely exams will be held in-person, similar to what happened the year before when the emergence of the delta variant coincided with the students’ exams.

Associate Professor of English at the Higher School of Technology in Laayoune, Limame Barbouchi, told Hespress EN in a statement that “the recent tougher sanitary precautions announced by the Moroccan government following the rapid spread of Omicron worldwide have brought back the possibility of running virtual classes and exams at the universities to guarantee pedagogical continuity.” “At the time being, as it is difficult to evaluate the pandemic situation in Morocco, most universities have modified their exam dates to have Fall semester exams on-campus before the Fall vacation,” he added. Barbouchi said that if there is an increase in positive cases, it is very likely that classes will be conducted online in the upcoming semesters to ensure the safety of faculty, staff, and students. As for remote exams, the Associate Professor said “online exams are debatable as they don’t guarantee the credibility of students’ answers,” he noted, adding, “However, according to different academic studies and researches, including an international conference on Distance Education we held at Higher School of Technology in Laayoune last June, online exams can be possibly held making use of the recent technological developments to reduce the cheating online as possible as one can.” In response to Hespress EN’s question about recent technological advancements, the Associate Professor gave recommendations, such as: “the use of HD webcams to be imposed on students, new applications to help teachers create questions randomly, as well as new technology to allow students to control their access to other websites when taking online exams, such as Moodle,” he continued. Currently, Moroccan universities haven’t decided on the format of exams but have begun instituting stricter measures to curtail the spread of the Omicron variant, including the control of students’ identities and vaccination passes.
